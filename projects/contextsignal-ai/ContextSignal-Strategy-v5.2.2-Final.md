## 1. The Strategic Shift

The original ContextSignal.ai plan was built around a classic affiliate content model: automated AI-generated tool reviews, monetised through affiliate commissions and display ads, with traffic driven primarily by Google organic search.

This revised strategy pivots to a **context engineering approach**. Instead of reviewing tools generically, ContextSignal becomes a platform that teaches professionals how to use AI within their specific roles. The site is organised by audience rather than tool category, with content that shows people how to integrate AI into real workflows.

This shift solves three critical problems with the original model:

- **Differentiation:** Role-specific AI workflow content is far less saturated than generic tool listicles.
- **Google resilience:** Practical workflow guides — especially those with original templates, screenshots, role-specific examples, and downloadable assets — are harder for AI Overviews to fully answer, preserving click-through rates. This resilience is not automatic; it comes from the quality of the editorial and flagship tiers, not from the automated tier.
- **Revenue diversification:** The audience-first model unlocks digital products, newsletters, sponsorships, and community revenue beyond just affiliate links and display ads.

| Dimension | Original Plan | Revised Plan |
|---|---|---|
| Organising principle | Tool categories (AI Writing, Coding, etc.) | Audience verticals (PMs, Sales, Designers, etc.) |
| Content focus | Tool reviews and comparisons | AI workflows, prompt libraries, tool recommendations in context |
| Primary revenue | Affiliate commissions + display ads | Affiliate + digital products + newsletter + sponsorships |
| Google dependency | ~90% of traffic | Target: <50% of revenue from organic search |
| Content quality | 100% AI-generated, spot-checked | 3 tiers: automated (draft-first), editorial, flagship |
| Owner time | 1–2 hours/week | 4–6 hours/week (first 6 months), then 2–3 hours/week |

## 2. One Metric That Matters (Per Phase)

Every phase has many metrics. But to avoid drifting into "publish more" as the default behaviour, each phase anchors on one metric that determines whether you're building a business or just a content archive.

| Phase | OMTM | Target | Why This Metric |
|---|---|---|---|
| Weeks 1–6 | Email subscribers from PM + Sales lead magnets | 100+ | Proves the content attracts a real audience willing to give you their email — the foundation for every non-Google revenue stream. |
| Weeks 7–12 | Search Console traction (impressions + clicks) + list growth cadence | 500+ impressions/week, 300+ subscribers | Proves Google is indexing and surfacing your content, and that the list is growing steadily without paid acquisition. |
| Month 4–6 | First digital product conversion rate | 2–5% of email list purchases | Proves the audience will pay you directly — the unlock for revenue diversification. |
| Month 7–12 | Monthly revenue from non-Google sources | 50%+ of total revenue | Proves the business can survive a Google algorithm shift. |

If the OMTM isn't tracking, stop publishing more content and fix the funnel, the lead magnet, or the targeting first.

## 3. Stop Rules

These prevent waste. If a metric isn't tracking, the response is never "publish more" — it's diagnose and fix.

| Trigger | Response |
|---|---|
| Week 3: fewer than 20 subscribers total | Stop publishing. Fix lead magnet offer + CTA placement + distribution before creating more content. |
| Week 6: fewer than 100 subscribers | Rewrite both lead magnets. Add 2 distribution channels (community posts + partnership outreach). Test CTA copy and placement. |
| Money pages getting impressions but no clicks | Fix titles and meta descriptions. Improve above-the-fold CTA. Check that the page delivers on the search intent immediately. |
| Email signups happening but open rates below 25% | Deliverability audit: check DKIM/SPF/DMARC. Review subject lines. Segment and resend to unopened. Suppress unengaged subscribers (90+ days no opens). |
| Editorial posts getting traffic but no signups | CTA is invisible or irrelevant. Test inline CTA placement (not just end-of-post). Test different lead magnet angles. |
| Affiliate links getting clicks but no conversions | Review the landing page experience. Check cookie duration. Consider whether the tool recommendation is genuinely strong or just convenient. |
| Automation pipeline producing duplicate/thin content | Tighten deduplication rules. Reduce volume. Review prompt templates for specificity. |
| Week 6: fewer than 10 total replies across welcome sequences + community posts (zero external validation) | The market isn't engaging. Simplify lead magnets. Increase community engagement quality (answer threads daily for 10 min). Send 5 direct partnership outreach messages offering your signature framework as a free resource. Replies are the earliest market signal you can get — if nobody's responding, the positioning or offer needs work. |
| Week 6: 100+ email subscribers but fewer than 10 waitlist signups combined across PM + Sales (signups but no waitlist intent) | The list is growing but nobody wants to buy. Tighten the lead magnet → product bridge: add a "what's next" page immediately after the lead magnet download that pitches the waitlist with concrete bullets and a clear outcome. Mention the waitlist more clearly in Emails 4 and 5. Make the waitlist offer more specific — list exactly what's included and what problem it solves. Email subscribers alone can be freebie-seekers; waitlist signups are the real demand signal. |

### A/B Testing Rule

When a stop rule triggers, you need to change something. To avoid changing everything at once and learning nothing:

- **Test one variable at a time:** CTA copy, CTA placement, lead magnet headline, or lead magnet offer. Never change two at once.
- **Minimum sample:** Run each test for at least 200 pageviews on the pages using that CTA, or 2 weeks — whichever comes first.
- **Keep a "winning CTA" log:** A simple doc with date, what was tested, which variant won, and the conversion rate difference. This prevents re-testing things you've already resolved and builds institutional knowledge about what your audience responds to.

## 4. Audience Verticals

The site is structured around **eight audience verticals**. Each vertical is a mini-publication targeting a specific professional role, with its own content pipeline, keyword strategy, affiliate programs, and digital product potential.

**Critical execution principle: do not launch all verticals at once.** The model works with fewer verticals and is more likely to succeed with focused depth than scattered breadth. The launch is phased across three waves.

---

### Wave 1 — Core Pair (Weeks 1–6)

Launch with only two verticals. These share tool overlap (Notion, Monday.com, HubSpot all serve both audiences), similar content patterns, and the strongest combination of affiliate revenue potential and content feasibility. All systems, editorial standards, and automation rules get proven on these two before expanding.

#### 1. AI for Project Managers

**Positioning:** *AI workflows that turn PM busywork — docs, updates, risk tracking — into a 30-minute weekly system.*

Project managers are early AI adopters with clear pain points: status updates, sprint planning, stakeholder reporting, risk tracking. They use tools with strong affiliate programs and are willing to pay for productivity gains. This is the easiest vertical to build "workflow + templates" content for, and affiliate links fit naturally.

**Target audience:** Product managers, project managers, Scrum Masters, PMO leads, operations managers.

**Core topics:** AI-powered sprint planning, automated status reports, AI for stakeholder communication, prompt templates for project documentation, risk assessment with AI, meeting notes automation.

**Key affiliate programs:** Notion ($8–10/referral + recurring), Monday.com (25% recurring), ClickUp (20% recurring), Asana, Linear, Jira AI features.

**Digital product angle:** "The AI-Powered PM Toolkit" — prompt library + workflow automations + tool comparison matrix. Price: $39–49.

**Signature framework (ship by Week 6):** "The AI PM OS" — a single visual workflow diagram showing how AI plugs into the PM daily cycle (standup → sprint planning → status report → retrospective → stakeholder update), with a downloadable template for each stage. This becomes the brand asset that people screenshot, share, and reference.

**Canonical tool stack (6 tools — keep stable):**
1. **Notion** — AI-powered docs, sprint boards, knowledge base
2. **Monday.com** or **ClickUp** — project tracking with AI automations
3. **Slack** — team communication (AI summaries, workflow triggers)
4. **Google Workspace** — docs, sheets, slides with Gemini AI
5. **Otter.ai** or **Fireflies** — meeting transcription and action items
6. **Zapier** or **Make.com** — workflow automation connecting the stack

Every workflow guide references this baseline stack. This creates consistency across content, makes the `/tools/` directory manageable, and avoids constant tool sprawl. Canonical stacks are default recommendations; we introduce "specialist tools" only when they demonstrably outperform a canonical pick for a specific job.

#### 2. AI for Sales Professionals

**Positioning:** *AI workflows that turn research and personalisation into repeatable outbound — without sounding robotic.*

The highest-value vertical on the list. Sales teams are among the heaviest AI adopters — industry data shows that sales teams using AI grew revenue at significantly higher rates than those without, and reps save an estimated 2+ hours per day on admin tasks. The affiliate programs are exceptionally lucrative, with CPA payouts reaching $50–200 per referral and recurring commissions up to 30%.

**Canonical tool stack (6 tools — keep stable):**

2. **Apollo.io** — prospecting, lead enrichment, outbound sequencing

Canonical stacks are default recommendations; we introduce "specialist tools" only when they demonstrably outperform a canonical pick for a specific job.

**Why Sales is in Wave 1:** A single HubSpot referral on a mid-tier plan could be worth nearly $2,900 in recurring commissions over a year. The audience naturally spends money on tools that promise revenue growth, which means higher conversion rates on affiliate links.

## 5. Topic Selection Doctrine

Random content kills niche sites. Every article published must fit one of three categories, with a strict allocation to prevent drift.

### Content Mix (Per Vertical)

| Category | Share | Content Type | Tier | Intent |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Workflow guides | 70% | Role + job-to-be-done ("How to use AI for sprint retrospectives") | Editorial + Flagship | Informational → email signup |
| Comparisons & tool stacks | 20% | High-intent buyer content ("Best AI tools for SDRs 2026") | Editorial only (must be human-reviewed) | Commercial → affiliate conversion |
| News & updates | 10% | Freshness content ("Notion AI March 2026 update") | Tier 1 automated | Informational → crawl freshness |

### Money Pages (Defined Explicitly)

These pages target commercial-intent keywords and carry affiliate links. They must always be Tier 2 (editorial) or Tier 3 (flagship) — never Tier 1 automated.

**PM vertical money pages:**
- "Best AI Tools for Project Managers 2026"
- "Notion AI vs Monday AI vs ClickUp AI for PMs"
- "AI-Powered PM Toolkit" (product landing page)
- "Best Free AI Tools for Project Management"

**Sales vertical money pages:**
- "Best AI Sales Tools 2026"
- "HubSpot AI Workflows for SDRs"
- "Best AI Tools for Cold Email Outreach"
- "AI Sales Playbook" (product landing page)

### Topic Backlog Requirement

Before publishing any content in a vertical, build a topic backlog of 25 topics tagged by:
- Tier (1, 2, or 3)
- Category (workflow 70% / comparison 20% / news 10%)
- Intent (informational / commercial / navigational)
- Priority (publish first / publish later / backlog)

This prevents random content and ensures every post serves a clear purpose.

## 6. Conversion System (Per Vertical)

This is the difference between "a lot of content" and "a business." Every vertical needs a defined funnel before content production begins.

### Funnel Components (Required Per Active Vertical)

#### 1. Lead Magnet

A free downloadable asset offered in exchange for an email address. Must be genuinely useful — not a teaser for the paid product.

**PM lead magnet:** "50 AI Prompt Templates for Project Managers" — a PDF with ready-to-use prompts for sprint planning, status reports, risk assessment, stakeholder updates, and meeting summaries.

**Sales lead magnet:** "The AI Cold Email Playbook: 30 Prompt Templates for SDRs" — a PDF with prompt templates for prospecting research, personalised outreach, follow-up sequences, and objection handling.

#### 2. Primary CTA (On Every Post)

Every post in a vertical has one consistent CTA — not a different ask each time. The CTA appears at the end of every post and optionally as an inline callout within longer articles.

**PM CTA:** "Get the free PM prompt pack → 50 AI templates for sprint planning, status reports, and stakeholder updates."

**Sales CTA:** "Get the free AI cold email playbook → 30 prompt templates for prospecting, outreach, and follow-ups."

#### 3. Welcome Sequence (5 Emails)

Triggered immediately after lead magnet download. The goal for the first 5 emails is trust and engagement, not revenue. Optimise for replies and opens, not clicks on affiliate links.

**Email 1 (Day 0) — Deliver + connect:** Deliver the lead magnet. One sentence of context. "Reply and tell me what AI tool you're currently using for [PM work / sales outreach]." The reply prompt is critical — it trains inbox providers that your emails are wanted, improving deliverability for every future send.

**Email 2 (Day 2) — Best workflow tip:** One high-value workflow tip. Link to the best flagship article for this vertical. "This is the workflow I recommend starting with." Pure value, no pitch.

**Email 3 (Day 5) — Soft tool mention:** A comparison or educational piece. Mention a tool naturally within the workflow ("For this step, I use Notion AI — here's why it works well for sprint docs"). Soft, contextual. Not a hard affiliate pitch. The goal is still trust-building.

**Email 4 (Day 8) — Reader questions:** A "what readers are asking" email. Answer 2–3 common questions collected from the lead magnet download form and replies. Builds authority and engagement. Include a link to a relevant editorial post.

**Email 5 (Day 12) — Waitlist or product intro:** If the product exists: introduce it with a brief "here's what's inside" and a link. If it doesn't exist yet: introduce the waitlist with one question — "What would make this worth buying for you?" Keep friction low. Never hard-sell in a welcome sequence.

#### 4. Product Waitlist / Pre-Sell Page

Set up from Week 1, even before the product exists. Collects interest and validates demand.

A simple landing page: headline, 3–5 bullet points on what the product will include, an email field ("Join the waitlist — launching [month]"), and a 2-question form: "What's your biggest challenge using AI in [PM work / sales]?" and "What would you pay for a toolkit that solves it? ($19 / $39 / $49 / $69+)."

This data directly informs product pricing and content.

## 7. Distribution Doctrine

Content without distribution is invisible. Even with strong SEO fundamentals, Google organic takes months to build. You need repeatable acquisition channels from Week 1 that don't require going viral.

### Distribution Mix (Weeks 1–12)

| Channel | Share | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| SEO | 40% | Search Console learning loop: track impressions → optimise titles/meta → double down on gaining keywords. Slow but compounding. |
| Communities | 30% | Reddit (r/projectmanagement, r/sales, r/artificial), niche Slack/Discord groups, LinkedIn groups. Value-first posts: share a workflow tip, link to the full guide only when it genuinely adds value. Never spam. |
| Partnerships | 20% | Tool newsletters (Notion, Monday.com, ClickUp often feature content about their products), small creators in PM/sales niches, podcast guest appearances. Offer genuine value: original data, frameworks, templates. |
| Repurposing | 10% | Turn flagship posts into short LinkedIn carousels, Twitter/X threads, and newsletter content. One flagship post = 3–5 distribution assets. |

### Distribution Rule

Every flagship post must have at least 3 distribution shots before it's considered "launched":
1. A community post (Reddit, LinkedIn group, or niche forum) — value-first, not promotional
2. A social post (LinkedIn carousel, Twitter/X thread, or short-form content)
3. Featured in the next newsletter issue

Editorial posts should have at least 1 distribution shot (community or social).

Tier 1 automated posts get no active distribution — they exist for SEO freshness only.

### Community Engagement Standards

- **Value-first, always.** Share a useful tip, answer a question, or provide a framework. Link to the article only when it genuinely extends the value.
- **No drive-by posting.** Engage in the community before and after posting. Reply to comments. Answer follow-up questions.
- **One link per post maximum.** If the community rules prohibit links, share the content without linking.
- **Never use the same copy across communities.** Tailor each post to the community's norms and audience.
- **3:1 ratio rule:** For every 1 post that includes a link, make 3 contributions with zero links (answers, comments, helpful replies). This is easy to follow and keeps you safe from bans and reputational damage.

### Lightweight Backlink Plan

SEO will be slow without some inbound links. Keep it minimal, non-spam, and aligned with your signature frameworks.

**Share kit per flagship framework:** Each signature framework (AI PM OS, SDR Personalisation Stack) gets a dedicated "share kit" — a page with the framework graphic, a short embed-ready description, and an attribution link back to the full guide. This makes it effortless for other creators to reference your work.

**Outreach targets (Weeks 2–6, aligned with partnership outreach):**
- Tool blogs (Notion, Monday.com, ClickUp often accept guest contributions or link to useful resources about their product)
- Small newsletters in the PM / sales / productivity space
- "Resources" and "useful links" pages on relevant blogs and communities
- Other content creators who cover AI workflows (offer a framework graphic in exchange for a mention)

**Goal:** 2 backlinks per month in months 1–3. This is modest but enough to help indexing speed and early ranking signals. Quality over quantity — one link from a relevant tool blog is worth more than 20 from directories.

## 8. Three-Tier Content Strategy

Every vertical follows the same three-tier content model. The tiers differ in production effort, SEO purpose, and revenue function.

| Tier | Volume | Production | Purpose | Revenue Role |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Automated | 5–10 total/week (across all active verticals) | Claude API + Make.com pipeline → **drafts only** | SEO freshness, long-tail coverage (news/updates = 10% of mix) | Display ads only. No affiliate links unless human-reviewed. |
| Editorial | 2–3/week per active vertical | AI-drafted, human-edited (30–60 min each) | Topical authority, workflow guides (70%) + comparisons (20%) | Contextual affiliate links, email list growth via CTA |
| Flagship | 1–2/month per active vertical | Personally crafted (2–4 hours each) | Brand authority, lead magnets, link-worthy content | Digital product conversion, sponsorship hooks, email growth |

### Tier 1: Automated Content — Rules & Quality Gate

This is where the Make.com + Claude API pipeline earns its keep. But volume without quality is a liability, not an asset.

**Content types:** Pricing update posts, "What's new in [Tool] this month", AI news digests by role, prompt tip of the day. These are the 10% "news & updates" category only.

**Automation pipeline:**
1. Make.com monitors Google Trends + Reddit + Product Hunt for AI tool mentions relevant to active verticals
2. Topic deduplication check — pipeline skips topics already covered (same role + same task + same tool set = duplicate)
3. Claude API generates the article using vertical-specific templates
4. Article is pushed to WordPress as a **draft** (never auto-publish)
5. Weekly batch review (20–30 minutes): scan drafts, approve the best, discard or merge duplicates

**Automation rules (non-negotiable):**
- All Tier 1 posts publish as **drafts by default** — no auto-publishing
- **Publish caps:** Maximum 2 Tier 1 publishes per week during Weeks 2–6. Only increase after Week 6 if Search Console confirms indexing and no cannibalisation issues.
- **No affiliate links** in any post that hasn't had at least 5 minutes of human review
- No posts targeting money keywords (e.g., "best [tool] for [role]") without editorial review — these are always Tier 2+
- Dedupe topics before generation to avoid cannibalisation
- Target: 5–10 total automated drafts per week across all active verticals (not per vertical)
- Publish selectively — generating 10 drafts and publishing 5–7 is better than publishing all 10

**Fact verification protocol for Tier 1:**
- All pricing claims must include a "last verified: [date]" note
- Link to vendor pricing page or note "pricing changes frequently — check [tool].com for current pricing"
- Feature claims must reference a specific product version or update date
- When in doubt, soften the claim: "at the time of writing" or "as of [date]"

### Tier 2: Editorial Content

This is the middle layer that builds real topical authority. AI generates the first draft, but you spend 30–60 minutes editing each piece.

**Content types:** Workflow guides (70% of mix), detailed tool comparisons and tool-stack pages (20% of mix), tool reviews with screenshots, prompt libraries for specific tasks, "I tested X for a week" posts.

**Quality standard — every editorial post must include at least one of these original elements:**
- A screenshot from actual tool usage
- A personal observation or genuine opinion
- A specific workflow example with real steps
- Original benchmark data or a timing comparison
- A downloadable template, checklist, or asset

**Every editorial post must include:**
- The vertical's primary CTA (lead magnet)
- At least 1 internal link to the hub page
- At least 1 internal link to a related post
- A "last updated" or "last verified" date on any pricing or feature claims
- At least 1 distribution shot (community or social)

### Tier 3: Flagship Content

These are the pieces that define the brand. One or two per month per active vertical, each one substantial enough to be shared, linked to, and referenced.

**Content types:** "The Complete Guide to AI for Project Managers (2026)", original framework posts (including the signature framework), data-driven analyses, tool comparison matrices, email course content, chapters that eventually become digital products.

**Every flagship post must include:**
- The vertical's primary CTA
- A downloadable asset (this is the lead magnet or a standalone resource)
- Multiple original elements (screenshots, frameworks, data)
- Internal links to hub + at least 3 related posts
- At least 3 distribution shots (community + social + newsletter)

## 9. Content Refresh Policy

Stale content loses rankings and reader trust. Define refresh cycles by content type and stick to them.

| Content Type | Refresh Cycle | What Gets Updated |
|---|---|---|
| Money pages (comparisons, "best X" posts) | Every 60–90 days | Pricing, features, new tool entrants, "last verified" dates, screenshots if UI changed |
| Tool directory pages | Every 90–120 days | Pricing, key features, alternatives list, "last verified" date |
| Workflow guides | When major tool UX changes | Workflow steps, screenshots, prompt templates if tool behaviour changed |
| Flagship / evergreen | Every 6 months | Stats, tool recommendations, downloadable assets, internal links to newer content |
| Tier 1 automated | No refresh | If outdated, unpublish rather than update. Not worth the maintenance. |

### What Cannot Be Claimed

- No tool is "the best" without specifying the context, use case, and criteria

- No financial advice, trading signals, or return projections (Traders vertical)

- No unverified statistics — if a stat can't be sourced, don't use it

### Editorial QA Checklist (10 Items)

Use this checklist before publishing any Tier 2 or Tier 3 post:

1. Does the title clearly identify the role AND the task/topic?
2. Does the post match the topic doctrine (workflow 70% / comparison 20% / news 10%)?
3. Is there at least one original element (screenshot, personal observation, data)?
4. Are all pricing and feature claims tagged with "last verified: [date]"?
5. Are affiliate links contextual and natural (not forced)?
6. Is the vertical CTA present (lead magnet callout)?
7. Does the post link to the hub page and at least 1 related post?
8. Does the post avoid all "cannot claim" violations (including no results claims in Sales)?
9. Is the meta description written and under 155 characters?
10. Is the post assigned to the correct vertical category with proper URL slug?

## 11. Attribution System

### Measurement Instrumentation Checklist (Week 1)

Set this up before publishing any content. Without it, the weekly dashboard and stop rules are guesswork.

**GA4 key events (configure in Week 1):**
- `lead_magnet_signup` — fires when someone submits an email on a lead magnet form. Track which vertical and which page triggered it.
- `waitlist_signup` — fires when someone joins a product waitlist. Separate from lead magnet signups — this is the demand signal.
- `affiliate_click` — fires on any click to a `/go/*` Pretty Link. Track as GA4 event with parameters: `tool` (e.g., "notion"), `vertical` (e.g., "pm" or "sales"), `source_page` (the `page_path` where the click happened), and `link_path` (the Pretty Link path, e.g., "/go/notion-pm"). The `link_path` makes analysis easier than parsing tool/vertical separately.

**Beehiiv source capture:**
- Ensure signup forms preserve UTM parameters (or at minimum store the referring URL). This is how you know whether a subscriber came from a Reddit post, a newsletter mention, or a Google search.
- If Beehiiv doesn't natively pass UTMs, add hidden fields to the signup form that capture `utm_source`, `utm_medium`, and `utm_campaign` from the URL.

**Search Console (Week 1):**
- Submit XML sitemap (Rank Math generates this automatically).
- Verify canonical URLs are correct on all hub pages — these are your most important SEO assets and must not have conflicting canonicals.
- Request indexing for hub pages manually via the URL Inspection tool to accelerate initial crawling.

Without this instrumentation, the "Weekly Dashboard (5 Minutes)" check has no reliable data to work with.

From day 1, track what works so decisions are data-driven, not vibes.

## 12. Email Deliverability & List Health

A newsletter is only an asset if it reaches inboxes. These requirements are non-negotiable.

### Technical Setup (Week 1)

- **Custom sending domain** in Beehiiv — send from `newsletter@contextsignal.ai` or `ale@contextsignal.ai`, not from a Beehiiv subdomain
- **DKIM, SPF, and DMARC records** configured in DNS (Kinsta or domain registrar) — Beehiiv provides the records, you add them
- **Verify setup** with a tool like mail-tester.com — target a score of 9/10 or higher before sending the first email

### List Health Rules (Ongoing)

- **Reply prompt in Email 1** of every welcome sequence — replies signal to inbox providers that the email is wanted. Already included in the sequence design.
- **Suppress unengaged subscribers** — anyone who hasn't opened an email in 90 days gets moved to a "re-engagement" segment. Send one re-engagement email ("Still interested?"). If no open, remove from active list.
- **Monitor bounce rate** — hard bounces removed automatically by Beehiiv. If soft bounce rate exceeds 2%, investigate.
- **Monitor spam complaints** — if complaints exceed 0.1%, pause and investigate subject lines, frequency, and content relevance.
- **Double opt-in** — optional for now, but switch to double opt-in if spam complaints rise above 0.05% or if list growth comes from sources with low intent.
